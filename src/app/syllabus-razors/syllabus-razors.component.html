Philosophical Razors

In philosophy, a razor is a principle or rule of thumb that allows one to eliminate ("shave off") unlikely explanations for a phenomenon, or avoid unnecessary actions.

Principle of Charity

In philosophy and rhetoric, the principle of charity or charitable interpretation requires interpreting a speaker's statements in the most rational way possible and, in the case of any argument, considering its best, strongest possible interpretation.
Never attribute to malice that which can be adequately explained by stupidity.

Occam's Razor

Simpler explanations are more likely to be correct; avoid unnecessary or improbable assumptions.

Hitchens' Razor

What can be asserted without evidence can be dismissed without evidence.

Hume's Guillotine

What ought to be cannot be deduced from what is. "If the cause, assigned for any effect, be not sufficient to produce it, we must either reject that cause, or add to it such qualities as will give it a just proportion to the effect."

Newton's Flaming Laser Sword

If something cannot be settled by experiment or observation, then it is not worthy of debate.

Sagan Standard

Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence.
